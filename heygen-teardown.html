❌ Before

Subject: Our newest avatar model is here! Here's what it changes for you.


Company-first framing. Every product launch email uses this exact structure. Zero curiosity gap. The reader has no reason to open it over the next email.

✅ After

Subject: Your AI avatar just stopped looking like an AI avatar.


Reader-first. Describes a change in their reality, not a company announcement. Creates a specific open loop — they have to click to find out how their avatar changed.

The 7 upgrades — and why they work
1 · Subject line: announcement → reader reality
"Our newest avatar model is here" leads with HeyGen's news. "Your AI avatar just stopped looking like an AI avatar" describes a change in the reader's world. Subject lines that open a loop about the reader's situation get opened. Subject lines that announce company news get skipped.
2 · Hook: generic superlative → specific scene
"The most advanced AI avatar model in the world" is a claim HeyGen makes about itself. "Creators publishing videos indistinguishable from real footage — same face, same voice, different outfit, different location, different language" is a scene the reader can picture. What you can picture, you can want.
3 · 4-step list → consequence narrative
Instructions tell the reader what to do. They don't tell them why to do it today. The rewrite establishes the value first — no more re-recording, instant output, any language — before any action is implied. The to-do list assumes motivation the reader hasn't been given yet.
4 · "#1 rated" buried → surfaced into stat card
HeyGen's own video thumbnail said "Rated #1 most realistic avatars" — but as image text, invisible to most readers and all email clients that block images. The rewrite pulls that claim into a stat card where it earns credibility. Social proof that exists but isn't visible might as well not exist.
5 · Zero social proof → creator outcome installed
The original email has no third-party voice anywhere. The rewrite adds a creator quote describing a specific, verifiable outcome: 12 videos, 4 outfits, 3 languages, one 15-second setup clip. Named, specific, and impossible to dismiss as marketing copy.
6 · No consequence → cost of inaction made explicit
"Creators who haven't set up Avatar V are still re-recording every time their script, outfit, or location changes. That gap compounds every week." The cost of inaction is the most underused conversion lever in product emails. Describing what faster-moving peers are doing creates urgency without false scarcity.
7 · Passive CTA → owned outcome with time anchor
"Create your Avatar V now →" describes an action. "Build your Avatar V in 15 seconds →" answers the reader's hidden objection — how long will this take? — before they ask it. Ownership CTAs describe what the reader gets on the other side of the click, not just the act of clicking.
Conversion hook
"Your AI avatar just stopped looking like an AI avatar" — this subject line works because it implies the reader already has an avatar that looked like AI, and something just changed without them doing anything. It creates a before/after in their mind before they even open the email. The curiosity gap is specific, personal, and resolves only by clicking.
